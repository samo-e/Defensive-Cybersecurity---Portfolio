## B16 – Survey the current state-of-the-art solutions in cybersecurity

### Objective

The objective of this activity was to survey current state-of-the-art cybersecurity solutions in a focused and evidence-based way. Instead of simply listing products that companies claim are “the best,” I scoped the activity around **modern defensive cybersecurity solutions**, especially those used for detection, prevention, identity protection, and automated vulnerability discovery. A solution was considered state-of-the-art only if it was supported by credible evidence such as independent evaluations, public research, government frameworks, or reproducible testing rather than only marketing claims.

---

### Scope

For this activity, I focused on four major areas of modern cybersecurity: **endpoint detection and response (EDR/XDR), zero trust security, AI-assisted vulnerability discovery, and modern cybersecurity risk frameworks**. These areas were selected because they represent current priorities in cybersecurity and are widely discussed in both industry and government guidance.

State-of-the-art in this context means a solution or approach that is not just new, but also shows strong practical performance, measurable outcomes, and adoption in real-world security environments. For example, an EDR product claiming to be advanced is not enough by itself. It should be tested against realistic attacker behaviour, such as through MITRE ATT&CK evaluations. Similarly, AI cybersecurity tools should be judged by their ability to detect or patch real vulnerabilities in controlled evaluations, not only by vendor claims.

---

### Findings

One important state-of-the-art area is **EDR/XDR**, which stands for Endpoint Detection and Response or Extended Detection and Response. These tools monitor devices and systems for suspicious behaviour, collect security telemetry, and help security teams respond to attacks. What makes EDR/XDR more advanced than traditional antivirus is that it focuses on attacker behaviour rather than only known malware signatures. MITRE ATT&CK Evaluations are useful here because they test security products against real-world adversary techniques in a transparent way. The 2024 MITRE ATT&CK Enterprise evaluation is a strong example of evidence-based assessment because it uses adversary emulation instead of relying only on vendor claims. ([ATT&CK Evaluations][1])

Another major SOTA direction is **zero trust security**. Traditional security often assumed that users or devices inside the network were trusted, but zero trust follows the idea of “never trust, always verify.” This means every user, device, and request must be continuously verified before access is granted. CISA’s Zero Trust Maturity Model provides a structured way for organisations to move from traditional security toward more advanced zero trust architecture. This includes identity, devices, networks, applications, workloads, and data as key areas of protection. ([CISA][2])

A newer state-of-the-art area is **AI-assisted vulnerability discovery and patching**. DARPA’s AI Cyber Challenge (AIxCC) is a strong example because it was designed as a large-scale competition where AI systems automatically analysed software, found vulnerabilities, and generated fixes. This is more credible than a normal company claim because the systems were tested in a competitive and structured environment. DARPA described AIxCC as a two-year challenge bringing together AI and cybersecurity teams to help secure critical software, and the 2025 results showed autonomous cyber reasoning systems becoming a serious emerging area in cybersecurity. ([darpa.mil][3])

Another important modern approach is using **cybersecurity frameworks and governance models** to manage risk across organisations. NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0, released in 2024, is not a single product, but it is considered important because it provides a structured way for organisations to manage cybersecurity risks. It includes outcomes that organisations can use to assess, prioritise, and communicate their cybersecurity efforts. This is relevant to state-of-the-art security because modern cybersecurity is not only about tools; it also includes governance, risk management, and repeatable processes. ([NIST Publications][4])

Overall, based on the evidence, the current SOTA in cybersecurity is not one single tool. It is more like a combination of advanced detection systems, zero trust access control, AI-assisted vulnerability analysis, and strong governance frameworks. The best solutions are those that can be independently evaluated, tested against realistic threats, and integrated into a wider security program.

---

### Mitigations / Recommendations

Organisations should be careful not to trust marketing claims alone when choosing security solutions. A good approach is to check whether a product or method has been tested through independent evaluations such as MITRE ATT&CK, supported by government guidance such as NIST or CISA, or validated through reproducible research. Security teams should also avoid relying on only one control. A modern security program should combine EDR/XDR, zero trust principles, vulnerability management, secure configuration, and continuous monitoring.

For smaller organisations, the most practical step is to follow recognised frameworks like NIST CSF 2.0 and gradually adopt stronger controls such as MFA, least privilege access, patch management, logging, and endpoint protection. For larger organisations, AI-assisted vulnerability discovery and automated response tools may become more useful, but they should still be reviewed by humans before being fully trusted.

---

### Reflection

This activity helped me understand that “state-of-the-art” in cybersecurity does not simply mean the newest product or the most popular tool. A real SOTA solution should have evidence behind it, such as independent testing, clear evaluation methods, or public research results. I also realised that cybersecurity is moving toward automation and AI, but human oversight and proper governance are still very important. The strongest security approach is not one tool, but a layered system that combines people, process, and technology.
