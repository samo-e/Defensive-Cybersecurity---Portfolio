## A18 – Discover two hallucination cases when using a generative AI system

### Objective

Identify two real examples of hallucination in generative AI systems. The goal is to understand how AI can sometimes produce incorrect or misleading information and what impact this can have.

---

### Findings

* A well-known real case happened in 2023 involving lawyer Steven A. Schwartz in the United States. He used a generative AI tool (ChatGPT) to help write a legal brief for a court case. The AI generated several case citations that looked real, but they were actually completely made up. The lawyer submitted these fake cases to the court, and when the judge asked for details, it was discovered that the cases did not exist. This resulted in penalties and became one of the most famous examples of AI hallucination in a professional setting. *(Source: Mata v. Avianca Airlines case, 2023)*

* Another real example involved Google’s AI chatbot Google Bard during its public demo in 2023. The AI gave an incorrect answer about the James Webb Space Telescope, claiming it was the first to take images of a specific exoplanet. In reality, this was incorrect, and the mistake was quickly pointed out by experts. This small error caused a lot of attention and even impacted public trust in the system. *(Source: Reuters, February 2023)*

---

### Mitigations (high-level recommendations)

To reduce the risk of AI hallucinations, it is important to always verify information using trusted sources, especially in professional or academic work. AI-generated content should be treated as a draft or starting point rather than a final answer. Developers should improve systems by adding fact-checking features and limiting confident responses when the model is uncertain. Overall, human verification is still necessary when using AI tools.

It highlights the importance of being careful and not fully trusting AI without verification.
