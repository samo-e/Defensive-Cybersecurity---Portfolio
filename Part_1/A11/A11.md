## A11 – Discover 5 unique access control devices

### Objective

Identify five different types of access control devices used in real-world systems. The goal is to understand how physical and digital access is controlled using different technologies.

### Method

1. Defined scope:

* Focused on commonly used access control devices in buildings, workplaces, and personal devices.
* Only used publicly available information (no testing or hacking).

2. Research approach:

* Looked at examples from offices, universities, apartments, and smartphones.
* Compared how each device works and what type of access it controls.

3. Selection criteria:

* Devices must be different from each other.
* Each device must use a unique method (e.g., card, fingerprint, code).

---

### Findings

#### 1. Key Card Reader (RFID/NFC)

* Used in hotels, offices, and universities.
* Users tap or swipe a card to unlock a door.

**How it works:**

* The card has a small chip.
* The reader scans the card and checks if it is allowed.

**Weakness:**

* Cards can be lost, stolen, or copied.

---

#### 2. Biometric Fingerprint Scanner

* Used in smartphones and secure buildings.

**How it works:**

* Scans a person’s fingerprint.
* Compares it with stored data to allow access.

**Weakness:**

* May fail if the finger is dirty or damaged.
* In rare cases, can be tricked with fake fingerprints.

---

#### 3. PIN Code Keypad

* Found in door locks, ATMs, and alarm systems.

**How it works:**

* User enters a secret number (PIN).
* If correct, access is granted.

**Weakness:**

* PINs can be guessed or seen by others (shoulder surfing).

---

#### 4. Facial Recognition System

* Used in modern phones and high-security areas.

**How it works:**

* Uses a camera to scan the face.
* Matches it with stored facial data.

**Weakness:**

* Can sometimes be fooled by photos or videos (depending on system quality).

---

#### 5. Smart Lock (Mobile App / Bluetooth)

* Used in smart homes and Airbnb properties.

**How it works:**

* Door unlocks using a phone app or Bluetooth signal.
* Can also allow remote access.

**Weakness:**

* Depends on phone security and internet connection.
* Can be hacked if poorly configured.

---

### Mitigations (high-level recommendations)

1. Use multi-factor authentication:

* Combine two methods (e.g., card + PIN) for better security.

2. Keep systems updated:

* Update software to fix security issues.

3. Protect credentials:

* Do not share cards, PINs, or access apps.

4. Monitor access logs:

* Track who enters and exits.

---

### Reflection

This task showed that access control is used everywhere, from phones to buildings. Each device has its own strengths and weaknesses. Simpler systems like PINs are easy to use but less secure, while advanced systems like biometrics offer better security but can still have flaws. Using multiple methods together gives stronger protection.
